## 3. Results

### 3.1 Cohort Characteristics

The final analytic cohort comprised 8,500 ICU stays with a primary stroke diagnosis extracted from MIMIC-IV v3.1 (Table 1). The median age was 68.0 years (IQR 57.0--78.0), with a near-equal gender distribution (50.1% female, 49.9% male). The racial composition reflected the source institution's catchment area, with White patients constituting the majority (58.8%), followed by Unknown/Declined (13.8%), and Black/African American (7.0%) (Figure 1).

Ischemic stroke was the most prevalent subtype, accounting for 4,398 patients (51.7%), followed by Other cerebrovascular events (1,987; 23.4%), intracerebral hemorrhage (ICH; 1,397; 16.4%), subarachnoid hemorrhage (SAH; 436; 5.1%), and transient ischemic attack (TIA; 282; 3.3%) (Figure 2). This distribution is consistent with epidemiological estimates in which ischemic events predominate, while hemorrhagic subtypes together account for approximately one-fifth of cases.

Comorbidity burden was substantial (Figure 3). Dyslipidemia was the most prevalent comorbidity (52.8%), closely followed by hypertension (51.7%), atrial fibrillation (31.2%), diabetes mellitus (28.1%), coronary artery disease (CAD; 25.2%), and chronic kidney disease (CKD; 17.8%). The comorbidity co-occurrence matrix revealed that the most frequent pairwise combination was hypertension with dyslipidemia (30.9% of the cohort), followed by dyslipidemia with atrial fibrillation (19.4%) and dyslipidemia with diabetes (19.0%) (Figure 4). Notably, hypertension and CKD co-occurred in only 0.4% of patients, a finding that likely reflects coding practices rather than true clinical dissociation.

Comorbidity profiles differed significantly across stroke subtypes (all p < 0.001; Table 2). Ischemic stroke patients had the highest prevalence of atrial fibrillation (38.7%), consistent with cardioembolic etiology, whereas SAH patients had the lowest rates of diabetes (14.4%), CKD (8.3%), and atrial fibrillation (15.6%), reflecting the distinct pathophysiology and younger demographic profile of aneurysmal hemorrhage (median age 62.0 years vs. 70.0 years for ischemic stroke). ICH patients exhibited the highest prevalence of hypertension (58.5%), aligning with hypertensive arteriopathy as a leading etiology.

In-hospital mortality was 14.8% overall (1,258 deaths). Mortality varied markedly by stroke subtype (Figure 5): ICH had the highest mortality rate (22.0%), followed by SAH (20.9%), ischemic stroke (17.0%), TIA (6.0%), and Other (4.9%) (p < 0.001). Stratification by mortality outcome (Table 3) revealed that non-survivors were significantly older (median 73.0 vs. 68.0 years; p < 0.001), more frequently male (52.5% vs. 49.4%; p = 0.046), and had higher comorbidity burden. Non-survivors demonstrated significantly higher prevalence of atrial fibrillation (42.7% vs. 29.3%), diabetes (33.6% vs. 27.1%), CKD (25.0% vs. 16.5%), and CAD (30.1% vs. 24.3%) (all p < 0.001). Admission laboratory values further distinguished the two groups: non-survivors had markedly higher glucose (median 140.0 vs. 120.0 mg/dL), elevated creatinine (1.1 vs. 0.9 mg/dL), higher INR (1.3 vs. 1.2), and lower hemoglobin (11.1 vs. 12.0 g/dL) (all p < 0.001) (Figure 6). Sodium levels did not differ between groups (p = 0.976). The median ICU length of stay was 2.7 days (IQR 1.3--5.9) overall, with non-survivors staying longer (3.4 vs. 2.6 days; p < 0.001). SAH patients had the longest ICU stays (median 5.7 days), consistent with the complexity of neurosurgical management in this population. Missing data rates were modest, ranging from 0% for demographic variables to 22.4% for INR, with laboratory values missing in 12.5--13.7% of cases.

### 3.2 Bayesian Network Structure

The Hill Climbing algorithm with BIC scoring learned a directed acyclic graph (DAG) comprising 31 edges across 17 clinical features (Figure 7). The maximum in-degree was constrained to 3 to prevent overfitting while preserving clinically meaningful dependencies. Several edges recovered by the algorithm corresponded to well-established clinical relationships. Age was identified as a parent node of atrial fibrillation, CKD, dyslipidemia, and mortality, reflecting the strong age-dependence of these conditions. The algorithm also discovered an edge from atrial fibrillation to stroke subtype, consistent with the role of atrial fibrillation as a cardioembolic risk factor for ischemic stroke. Hypertension was connected to ICH subtype probability and to laboratory creatinine, mirroring the pathophysiological link between chronic hypertension, hypertensive nephropathy, and hemorrhagic stroke. Diabetes was linked to glucose levels and to CKD, reproducing the well-characterized diabetic nephropathy pathway.

Among the more clinically nuanced dependencies, the network captured a connection between hemoglobin and mortality, reflecting the prognostic significance of anemia in critically ill stroke patients. Creatinine was linked to both CKD and mortality, consistent with the role of renal dysfunction as an independent predictor of poor stroke outcomes. The INR node was connected to atrial fibrillation (reflecting anticoagulant use) and to mortality (reflecting coagulopathy or liver dysfunction). Length of stay was influenced by stroke subtype and mortality, capturing the longer ICU stays associated with hemorrhagic subtypes and fatal outcomes. The correlation structure of the original data (Figure 8) was broadly consistent with the learned DAG: the strongest pairwise correlations in the heatmap (e.g., age--atrial fibrillation r = 0.36, creatinine--CKD r = 0.44, hemoglobin--INR r = -0.19) mapped onto edges in the Bayesian network.

### 3.7 Temporal Fidelity

The DoppelGANger (DGAN) component of the hybrid pipeline generated 72-hour ICU time-series conditioned on BN-produced static profiles. Temporal fidelity was assessed via trajectory comparison, DTW distances, and autocorrelation analysis.

**GCS trajectories by stroke subtype.** The synthetic GCS trajectories recapitulated the subtype-specific patterns observed in the real data (Figure 9). TIA patients maintained the highest mean GCS throughout the observation window (approximately 13.0--13.5), consistent with the typically mild neurological deficits in this group. Ischemic stroke and ICH patients exhibited lower initial GCS values (approximately 11.0--12.0) with modest improvement over 72 hours. SAH patients showed intermediate GCS values with wider confidence intervals, reflecting the heterogeneity of this subtype. The separation between subtypes was preserved in synthetic data, although confidence intervals were somewhat wider for the SAH group owing to its smaller sample size (5.1% of the cohort).

**Vital sign trajectories by mortality.** Real and synthetic vital sign trajectories were compared by mortality outcome (Figure 10). In both real and synthetic data, non-survivors exhibited persistently elevated heart rates (approximately 86--88 bpm vs. 78--80 bpm in survivors) and more labile systolic blood pressure trajectories over the first 48 hours. The characteristic early blood pressure drop followed by stabilization was reproduced in the synthetic trajectories, though the synthetic data slightly underestimated the magnitude of the initial blood pressure decline in non-survivors.
