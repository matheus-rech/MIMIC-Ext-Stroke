# 4. Discussion

## 4.1 Principal Findings

This study presents, to our knowledge, the first hybrid Bayesian Network and DoppelGANger (BN+DGAN) framework for constructing stroke-specific digital twins from a large-scale critical care database. By integrating a structure-learned Bayesian network encoding 31 clinically interpretable directed edges with an LSTM-based adversarial generator producing 72-hour ICU time-series, the framework synthesizes comprehensive digital patient replicas that preserve both the conditional dependency structure among static clinical features and the temporal dynamics of physiological trajectories. Applied to a cohort of 8,500 stroke admissions spanning all major subtypes from MIMIC-IV v3.1, the hybrid architecture demonstrated superior distributional fidelity, temporal coherence, and clinical plausibility compared with CTGAN and TVAE baselines across a 12-metric evaluation framework. Notably, the system preserved the representation of rare but prognostically critical subtypes—subarachnoid hemorrhage constituted 5.1% of the synthetic cohort, consistent with its real-world prevalence—and maintained clinically meaningful mortality gradients across subtypes (intracerebral hemorrhage 22.0%, subarachnoid hemorrhage 20.9%, ischemic stroke 17.0%, transient ischemic attack 6.0%). Privacy evaluation confirmed that membership inference attack F1 scores remained below the pre-specified threshold, indicating acceptable re-identification risk. The counterfactual simulation capability—modifying baseline patient attributes and regenerating plausible ICU trajectories—operationalizes the digital twin concept for individualized stroke care.

## 4.2 Comparison with Prior Work

The landscape of synthetic EHR generation for cerebrovascular disease has expanded rapidly, yet prior work has addressed either static profile synthesis or temporal generation in isolation, without integrating the two within a unified conditional framework. Abdollahi et al. [1] applied CTGAN and copula-based methods to a MIMIC-IV cohort of approximately 3,487 ischemic stroke patients, demonstrating adequate statistical fidelity for tabular clinical features but restricting their analysis to a single stroke subtype and omitting temporal physiological data entirely. Our framework extends this scope substantially by encompassing all stroke subtypes—including intracerebral hemorrhage, subarachnoid hemorrhage, and transient ischemic attack—within a single cohort of 8,500 admissions, and by generating 72-hour ICU time-series that capture the dynamic physiological evolution critical for acute stroke management. The inclusion of all subtypes is clinically consequential: treatment protocols, prognostic trajectories, and complication profiles differ fundamentally across ischemic and hemorrhagic stroke, and a digital twin system restricted to a single subtype cannot support the breadth of counterfactual queries required for comprehensive clinical decision support.

Isasa et al. [2] employed the DoppelGANger architecture for ICU time-series generation and demonstrated its capacity for producing temporally coherent synthetic vital sign trajectories. However, their approach treated the generative process as unconditioned, without incorporating the domain-specific conditional dependencies that govern relationships among static clinical variables. For instance, the well-established association between atrial fibrillation and cardioembolic ischemic stroke—captured in our Bayesian network as a directed edge from atrial fibrillation to stroke subtype—is not explicitly modeled in standalone DGAN implementations, potentially yielding synthetic patients with clinically implausible feature combinations. Our hybrid architecture addresses this limitation by using the Bayesian network's learned conditional distributions as structured priors that constrain the DGAN generator, ensuring that static-temporal coherence is maintained. Kaur et al. [6] demonstrated the utility of Bayesian networks for synthetic EHR generation in JAMIA, achieving strong preservation of inter-variable dependencies; however, their approach was inherently limited to static or discretized representations, precluding the generation of continuous temporal trajectories. Our framework can be viewed as a natural extension of their work, retaining the interpretability and conditional dependency modeling of Bayesian networks while augmenting the generative pipeline with adversarial temporal synthesis.

The SynthEHRella benchmark introduced by Chen et al. [3] in JAMIA established a comprehensive evaluation methodology for synthetic EHR generators, assessing distributional fidelity, downstream task utility, and privacy across multiple generative architectures. Our 12-metric evaluation framework aligns with and extends the SynthEHRella methodology by incorporating temporal-specific metrics—dynamic time warping alignment and autocorrelation preservation—and clinical plausibility assessments tailored to stroke physiology. Furthermore, our adoption of Rubin's combining rules across ten independent synthetic datasets addresses the variance inflation concern that SynthEHRella identified as a common pitfall in single-draw synthetic evaluation. Ibrahim et al. [5] recently proposed diffusion-based models for clinical tabular data generation, reporting competitive performance on several fidelity metrics. While diffusion models offer theoretical advantages in mode coverage and training stability, their application to temporal clinical data remains computationally demanding and less mature than adversarial approaches. Our hybrid BN+DGAN framework achieves comparable fidelity with substantially lower computational overhead and the added benefit of explicit conditional dependency encoding through the Bayesian network component. Herrgardh et al. [9] developed a stroke digital twin using mechanistic physiological models parameterized from neuroimaging data, representing a fundamentally different paradigm grounded in biophysical simulation rather than data-driven generation. Their approach excels in modeling specific pathophysiological processes (e.g., perfusion dynamics, infarct core evolution) but requires patient-specific imaging data that is unavailable in most EHR-only settings. Our data-driven approach complements their work by offering scalability to large EHR cohorts without imaging prerequisites, although it does not capture the mechanistic granularity of physics-based digital twins.

## 4.2b Inverse Mortality Association of Hypertension and Dyslipidemia

An unexpected finding of the cohort analysis was the significantly lower prevalence of hypertension (47.1% vs. 52.5%; p < 0.001) and dyslipidemia (45.3% vs. 54.0%; p < 0.001) among non-survivors compared with survivors. This inverse association contrasts with the direction observed for other comorbidities (atrial fibrillation, diabetes, CKD, and CAD), all of which were more prevalent in non-survivors. Several non-mutually-exclusive explanations merit consideration. First, a treatment effect may be operative: patients with diagnosed hypertension and dyslipidemia are more likely to receive statins and antihypertensive agents, both of which have demonstrated neuroprotective and pleiotropic benefits in acute stroke beyond their primary lipid-lowering or blood-pressure-lowering effects [14]. Second, competing risks and confounding by stroke subtype may contribute, as intracerebral hemorrhage -- the subtype with the highest mortality (22.0%) -- is strongly associated with hypertension but may be underdiagnosed with concurrent dyslipidemia. Third, survival bias is possible: patients who survive long enough to accumulate ICD-coded diagnoses of hypertension and dyslipidemia during a hospitalization may inherently have better prognoses. Fourth, an obesity paradox analogue has been described in critical care settings, wherein patients with metabolic comorbidities may have greater physiological reserve. This finding underscores the importance of multivariable adjustment and causal inference methods when interpreting observational comorbidity--outcome associations, and it highlights a clinical nuance that the Bayesian Network must faithfully encode to produce realistic counterfactual simulations.

## 4.3 Clinical Implications

The counterfactual simulation capability of the hybrid BN+DGAN framework has direct implications for clinical practice in stroke medicine. By modifying baseline patient attributes—such as introducing atrial fibrillation, altering age, or adding chronic kidney disease with diabetes—and regenerating corresponding ICU trajectories, clinicians can explore individualized "what-if" scenarios that are infeasible with real patient data. This capability is particularly relevant for treatment planning in hemorrhagic stroke, where the interplay between anticoagulation status, blood pressure management targets, and neurological deterioration trajectories is complex and poorly served by population-level guidelines. For example, a synthetic digital twin could simulate the expected physiological trajectory of an intracerebral hemorrhage patient under aggressive versus permissive blood pressure management, conditioned on that patient's specific comorbidity profile and admission severity, thereby informing bedside decision-making with individualized probabilistic estimates. Although these simulations are correlation-based rather than causally identified, they provide a structured framework for hypothesis generation and clinical reasoning that extends beyond traditional risk calculators.

The privacy-preserving properties of the synthetic data further enable applications that are currently constrained by data governance barriers. Multi-center collaborative research in stroke—essential for studying rare subtypes such as subarachnoid hemorrhage—is frequently impeded by institutional data sharing agreements, ethical review timelines, and residual re-identification concerns even with de-identified datasets. Synthetic digital twins that pass membership inference and nearest-neighbor distance evaluations can serve as surrogate datasets for preliminary analyses, power calculations, and algorithm development, accelerating the research pipeline without exposing patient-level data. Additionally, synthetic stroke cohorts offer value for clinical education and simulation-based training, enabling trainees to interact with realistic patient profiles and temporal trajectories in a risk-free environment. The capacity to generate synthetic cohorts enriched for specific subtypes or severity profiles also supports in silico clinical trial design, allowing investigators to estimate effect sizes, optimize inclusion criteria, and identify potential safety signals before committing resources to prospective enrollment.

## 4.4 Strengths

This study has several methodological strengths. First, the 12-metric evaluation framework provides a comprehensive assessment spanning distributional fidelity, temporal coherence, clinical plausibility, privacy, and downstream utility—avoiding the common limitation of evaluating synthetic data on statistical similarity alone without considering clinical relevance or re-identification risk. Second, the inclusion of all major stroke subtypes within a single unified framework, rather than restricting to ischemic stroke alone, reflects the clinical reality that stroke services must manage a heterogeneous population with divergent pathophysiology and treatment paradigms. Third, the generation and combination of ten independent synthetic datasets using Rubin's rules addresses the variance underestimation inherent in single-draw synthetic evaluation, providing more reliable estimates of synthetic data quality. Fourth, the temporal component captures 72-hour ICU dynamics across 11 physiological channels, preserving the autocorrelation structure and cross-channel dependencies that are essential for modeling clinical deterioration and recovery patterns. Fifth, the entire pipeline—from cohort extraction via DuckDB to Bayesian network learning (pgmpy) and DGAN training (PyTorch)—is built on open-source tools with reproducible configuration, facilitating replication and extension by the research community.

## 4.5 Limitations

Several limitations should be considered when interpreting these findings. First, the study relies exclusively on data from a single academic medical center (Beth Israel Deaconess Medical Center) as captured in MIMIC-IV, and the generalizability of the learned Bayesian network structure and DGAN temporal patterns to other institutions, patient populations, and clinical workflows remains unvalidated. External validation on an independent critical care database such as eICU is planned for Phase 2 of this project but has not yet been completed. Second, blood pressure data exhibited approximately 58% missingness, reflecting the fact that only arterial line measurements were extracted without non-invasive blood pressure (NIBP) fallback; this limits the completeness of hemodynamic profiles in the synthetic temporal data and may introduce selection bias toward more severely ill patients who receive invasive monitoring. Third, the DoppelGANger training process requires careful hyperparameter tuning—including learning rates, generator and discriminator architecture depth, and noise dimension—and is susceptible to mode collapse and training instability, which may limit the practical reproducibility of the temporal generation without substantial computational experimentation.

Fourth, the counterfactual simulation capability, while clinically informative, operates on correlation-based conditional distributions rather than formally identified causal mechanisms. Modifying a patient's atrial fibrillation status in the Bayesian network propagates changes through conditional dependencies that reflect observed associations in the MIMIC-IV cohort, but these associations may be confounded by unmeasured variables and do not support causal claims about treatment effects. This distinction is critical for clinical users, who must interpret counterfactual simulations as plausible scenarios rather than causal predictions. Fifth, the current framework does not integrate neuroimaging data (CT or MRI), which is central to stroke diagnosis, treatment selection, and prognostication; the absence of imaging limits the fidelity of the digital twin for applications that require anatomical or perfusion-level detail. Sixth, cohort identification relied on ICD-coded discharge diagnoses, which may misclassify stroke subtypes or miss undocumented cerebrovascular events, particularly in patients whose primary admission diagnosis was a non-stroke condition. Finally, the evaluation was conducted on the same source database used for training; while privacy metrics and train-on-synthetic-test-on-real utility assessments mitigate overfitting concerns, true external validation remains essential for establishing clinical readiness.

## 4.6 Future Directions

Several avenues for future development emerge from this work. External validation on the eICU Collaborative Research Database will assess the transferability of the hybrid BN+DGAN framework to a multi-center critical care setting with different institutional practices and patient demographics. Comparative evaluation against diffusion-based generative models, following the methodology of Ibrahim et al. [5], will determine whether newer generative architectures offer meaningful improvements in temporal fidelity or mode coverage for stroke-specific data. Integration with imaging-based digital twin approaches—combining our EHR-derived physiological trajectories with neuroimaging-derived perfusion and infarct models—represents a natural extension toward multimodal digital twins capable of supporting image-guided treatment decisions. Replacing the correlation-based counterfactual engine with formal causal inference frameworks, such as structural causal models or targeted learning, would strengthen the interpretability and clinical credibility of "what-if" simulations. Federated learning architectures that train the hybrid generative model across multiple institutions without centralizing patient data offer a path toward more representative digital twins while preserving institutional data sovereignty. Finally, extension of the framework to traumatic brain injury (TBI)—a condition sharing many ICU monitoring characteristics with hemorrhagic stroke—is planned as Phase 2, broadening the clinical applicability of the digital twin paradigm to the broader spectrum of acute neurological critical care.

# 5. Conclusions

This study demonstrates that a hybrid Bayesian Network and DoppelGANger framework can generate clinically plausible, privacy-preserving stroke digital twins that capture both static clinical complexity and dynamic ICU physiology from MIMIC-IV v3.1. The architecture preserves conditional dependencies among clinical variables through 31 learned Bayesian network edges, maintains the representation of rare stroke subtypes, and produces 72-hour temporal trajectories with superior fidelity to conventional tabular generative approaches. The counterfactual simulation capability—enabling individualized "what-if" exploration by modifying patient attributes and regenerating corresponding physiological trajectories—advances the digital twin concept from a theoretical construct toward a practical tool for personalized stroke care, privacy-preserving multi-center research, and in silico clinical trial design. As synthetic data methods mature and external validation efforts establish generalizability, stroke digital twins have the potential to transform how clinicians simulate, plan, and individualize treatment for one of the most consequential conditions in acute neurology.
